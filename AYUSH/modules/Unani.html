Unani Medicine
Greco-Arabic system of medicine — a tradition of balance, temperament, and natural therapies.

Introduction & History

Unani medicine (also called Unani tibb, Arabian medicine, or Islamic medicine) is a classical system of medicine with origins in the doctrines of ancient Greek physicians such as Hippocrates and Galen. The system was enriched and developed by Arab and Persian scholars — most notably Avicenna — and later spread across South Asia. Over centuries, healers (hakims) refined Unani with local medicinal plants and clinical experience.

Modes of Treatment

The Unani approach begins by restoring balance to external and internal factors — such as air, water, diet, and temperament — and strengthening the body's innate healing power (tabiyat). If lifestyle and regimen adjustments are insufficient, treatments include dietary therapy (ilaj-bi-ghiza), pharmacotherapy (ilaj-bi-dawa), and—less commonly—organotherapy (ilaj-bi-misla).

Unani pharmacopoeia is extensive; over two thousand remedies derived from herbs, minerals, and animal products are used singly or compounded to achieve the desired therapeutic effect. Classical preparation methods are still commonly employed to optimize potency and safety.

Regimental Therapies (Tadabeer)

Traditional regimens form an important part of Unani treatment and include practices intended to restore equilibrium in the body. Examples:

  • Dalak — therapeutic massage
  • Hammam — therapeutic baths/sauna
  • Karat — exercise and physical activity
  • Fasd — venesection (therapeutic bloodletting)
  • Hijamat — cupping therapy
  • Amat-e-kai — leech therapy

Surgical interventions (ilaj-bil-yad) are traditionally considered only when less invasive methods fail.

Principles of Unani Medicine

Unani medical thought emphasizes balance among several natural components. Central concepts include arkan (elements), mizaj (temperament), akhlat (humours), organs (aaza), vital spirit (arwah), faculties (quwa), and functions (afaal). Health arises when these elements remain in harmony and tabiyat (the body's self-regulating power) preserves equilibrium.

Elements, Temperament & Doctrine of Humours

Unani’s four basic elements — earth, water, fire, and air — combine to produce temperaments (mizaj): hot, cold, moist, and dry, and compound temperaments such as hot-dry or cold-moist. The doctrine of humours (akhlat) describes blood (dam), phlegm (balgham), yellow bile (safra), and black bile (sauda); an individual's health depends on a proper balance of these humours.

Modern Relevance & Research

In the 20th century, figures like Indian physician Ajmal Khan and phytochemist Salimuzzaman Siddiqui advanced scientific research on Unani remedies, isolating pharmacologically active compounds (e.g., reserpine from Rauwolfia). Such work helped validate some traditional uses and opened paths for integration with modern pharmacology.

Organizations such as the Central Council for Research in Unani Medicine (CCRUM) and recognition by the World Health Organization have supported standardization, clinical trials, and safety evaluations, strengthening Unani’s role in contemporary traditional medicine research and practice.

Did you know?
  • Greek roots: Unani draws heavily from Hippocrates and Galen.
  • Avicenna’s influence: The Canon of Medicine shaped Unani practice for centuries.
  • WHO recognition: Unani medicine received WHO attention in the 1970s.
  • Phytochemical breakthroughs: Ajmal Khan and Salimuzzaman Siddiqui helped validate herbal compounds used in Unani.
